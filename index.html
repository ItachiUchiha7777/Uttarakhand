Why Uttarakhand is Called Dev Bhoomi

The Land Where Gods Descended

Uttarakhand is reverently called Dev Bhoomi (Land of the Gods) because according to Hindu mythology, this is where the gods descended to Earth and where many significant events from the scriptures took place.

The Legend of Brahma's Creation

According to the Skanda Purana, Lord Brahma performed a great yajna (sacrificial ritual) in Uttarakhand to purify the land after the great flood. Pleased with his devotion, Lord Vishnu appeared and blessed the region, declaring it would forever be a sacred land where gods would reside.

The Descent of Ganga

The most famous legend associated with Uttarakhand is the descent of River Ganga. According to the story, King Bhagirath performed intense penance to bring the celestial Ganga to Earth to purify the souls of his ancestors. Lord Shiva agreed to break Ganga's fall by catching her in his matted locks, preventing the Earth from being destroyed by her force.

This is why Gangotri, where Ganga first touched Earth, and the surrounding regions are considered exceptionally sacred. The presence of Char Dham, numerous ancient temples, and the belief that the Himalayas are the abode of gods further cement Uttarakhand's status as Dev Bhoomi.

Char Dham of Uttarakhand

The four sacred pilgrimage sites in the Himalayas that hold immense spiritual significance for Hindus.

Yamunotri

The source of the Yamuna River and the seat of the goddess Yamuna, daughter of Surya (Sun God).

3,293 meters
History & Significance

According to legend, Yamuna was the daughter of Surya and sister of Yama, the god of death. Bathing in her waters is believed to protect from untimely death. The temple was built by Maharani Gularia of Jaipur in the 19th century.

Gangotri

The origin of the Ganges River, where Goddess Ganga descended from heaven to Earth.

3,100 meters
History & Significance

According to mythology, King Bhagirath performed penance here to bring Ganga to Earth to purify his ancestors' souls. The temple was built by a Gorkha commander, Amar Singh Thapa, in the 18th century. The actual source is at Gaumukh, 19 km away.

Kedarnath

One of the twelve Jyotirlingas, dedicated to Lord Shiva, and the highest among the Char Dham.

3,583 meters
History & Significance

According to legend, after the Mahabharata war, the Pandavas sought Lord Shiva's blessings to atone for their sins. Shiva eluded them and took refuge at Kedarnath as a bull. The temple is believed to have been built by the Pandavas and revived by Adi Shankaracharya.

Badrinath

Dedicated to Lord Vishnu, this temple is part of both Char Dham and Chota Char Dham.

3,133 meters
History & Significance

According to legend, Lord Vishnu meditated here under a Badri tree. The temple was established by Adi Shankaracharya in the 8th century. It's one of the 108 Divya Desams - holy shrines for Vaishnavites. The idol is believed to be self-manifested.

The Descent of Ganga: From Vishnu's Feet to Shiva's Locks

According to Hindu scriptures, the sacred River Ganga originally flowed in heaven. King Bhagirath performed intense penance to bring her to Earth to purify the souls of his ancestors.

Lord Brahma instructed that only Lord Shiva could break Ganga's mighty fall. Shiva agreed and captured Ganga in his matted locks, releasing her gently to Earth in seven streams.

The foremost stream became the Bhagirathi River which flows from Gangotri. This is why Shiva is also called Gangadhara - the bearer of Ganga.

Popular Attractions

Beyond the Char Dham, Uttarakhand offers numerous breathtaking destinations.

Hemkund Sahib

A Sikh gurudwara situated at an altitude of 4,632 meters, surrounded by seven mountain peaks and a glacial lake.

Sikh Pilgrimage 4,632m
More Information

According to Sikh tradition, Guru Gobind Singh, the tenth Sikh Guru, meditated here in his previous life. The gurudwara was built in the 20th century after being mentioned in the Guru's writings. The pristine lake beside it is considered sacred.

St. John's Church, Lansdowne

A beautiful colonial-era church in the peaceful hill station of Lansdowne, built in 1936.

Historical Site 1,700m
More Information

Lansdowne was a major garrison town for the Garhwal Rifles. The church was built during British rule and features beautiful stained glass windows. It remains an active place of worship and a reminder of Uttarakhand's colonial history.

Forest Research Institute (FRI)

A premier institution with magnificent colonial architecture, spread over 450 hectares.

Educational & Heritage Dehradun
More Information

Established in 1906 as Imperial Forest Research Institute, FRI is a UNESCO World Heritage Site candidate. Its Greco-Roman architecture is spread across a 450-hectare campus. It has six museums and contains rare botanical specimens.

Indian Military Academy (IMA), Dehradun

The premier officer training academy of the Indian Army, established in 1932.

Military Academy Dehradun
More Information

IMA was established in 1932 following a recommendation by the Indian Military College Committee. The academy trains officers for the Indian Army and has produced many war heroes. The Chetwode Building is its centerpiece, with the famous Chetwode Hall where the passing out parade takes place.

Shankaracharya Samadhi, Kedarnath

The final resting place of Adi Shankaracharya, the great Hindu philosopher who revived Hinduism.

Spiritual Site Kedarnath
More Information

Adi Shankaracharya is believed to have attained Mahasamadhi (conscious exit from the body) at Kedarnath at the young age of 32. He established the four Mathas (monasteries) in four corners of India and revived the Char Dham pilgrimage. His samadhi is located behind the Kedarnath temple.

Queen Teelu Rauteli - The Warrior Queen

A legendary folk heroine of Uttarakhand known for her bravery and wisdom during the 16th century.

She was the daughter of King Bhanu Pratap of Gangoli and married to King Dularam Singh of Mankot. When her husband was killed in battle, she took charge and defended her kingdom against invaders.

Her tales are an integral part of Kumaoni folklore, inspiring generations with her strategic mind and fearless nature. Folk songs celebrating her bravery are still sung in the region.

Regions of Uttarakhand

Uttarakhand is divided into two main cultural regions, each with its unique traditions and landscapes.

Garhwal Region

The western part of Uttarakhand, known for its majestic mountains, holy rivers, and spiritual significance.

  • Home to Char Dham pilgrimage sites
  • Famous hill stations: Mussoorie, Dhanaulti
  • Adventure sports: Trekking, skiing, river rafting
  • Major cities: Dehradun, Haridwar, Rishikesh
  • Districts: Chamoli, Dehradun, Haridwar, Pauri, Rudraprayag, Tehri, Uttarkashi
Spiritual Hub Adventure Destination

Kumaon Region

The eastern part of Uttarakhand, famous for its scenic beauty, lakes, and rich cultural heritage.

  • Famous for Nainital, Almora, Ranikhet
  • Beautiful lakes: Naini, Bhimtal, Sattal
  • Jim Corbett National Park - India's first national park
  • Unique Kumaoni cuisine and festivals
  • Districts: Almora, Bageshwar, Champawat, Nainital, Pithoragarh, Udham Singh Nagar
Lake District Cultural Heritage

History of Uttarakhand

Formation of Uttarakhand

Uttarakhand, formerly known as Uttaranchal, was carved out of Uttar Pradesh on November 9, 2000, becoming the 27th state of India.

The statehood movement gained momentum in the 1990s, driven by regional disparities and the need for better development in the hilly regions. The movement was largely peaceful but saw some violent incidents, most notably the Rampur Tiraha firing case in 1994.

The name was changed from Uttaranchal to Uttarakhand in 2007, reflecting the aspirations of the people and the historical and cultural significance of the region. "Uttarakhand" means "Northern Land" in Sanskrit.

Historical Timeline of Uttarakhand

Ancient Period

The region was part of the ancient Kedar Khand and mentioned in Hindu scriptures as Dev Bhoomi. It was ruled by the Kuninda Kingdom in the 2nd century BCE.

Medieval Period

The region was divided into the Garhwal Kingdom and Kumaon Kingdom. Both kingdoms had their own distinct cultures and rulers.

1815-1947

After the Anglo-Nepalese War, most of modern Uttarakhand became part of British India. The region was administered as part of the United Provinces.

Post-Independence

After India's independence in 1947, the region became part of the state of Uttar Pradesh.

1994-2000

The Uttarakhand movement gained momentum, demanding separate statehood for the hill regions of Uttar Pradesh.

9 November 2000

Uttarakhand was officially formed as the 27th state of India, initially named Uttaranchal.

2007

The state was renamed Uttarakhand, reflecting its historical and cultural identity.
